## [CHAPTER 1:10] ACT I — PLANT IT AND WALK AWAY
### *(Four perennials that reward neglect)*

[VISUAL: motion graphic — "ACT I · PLANT & WALK AWAY"]

### 1 · SEDUM (Stonecrop) — "The Succulent That Fools Everyone"

[VISUAL: thick sedum leaves, pink autumn blooms, container on sunny patio]

Start with the plant that looks the toughest and trips up lazy gardeners the most. Sedum stores water in those thick leaves — so everyone says *don't water it*. **Wrong — in year one.** New sedums need deep weekly water through their first June and July. After that? You can genuinely ignore them for years.

Fall color, pollinators, containers or beds. Zones **3–10**. Starter plant: **$5–8**. Lifespan: **15–20 years**. That's less than fifty cents a year.

### 2 · DAYLILY — "Tough After One Summer"

[VISUAL: orange daylily clump, neighbor's established bed vs new planting]

The classic "tough as nails" plant — **but only after year one**. First summer: deep water once or twice a week in heat. Year two onward: divide clumps every few years and get free plants. Full sun to part shade. Zones **3–9**. **$6–12** once → flowers for **20+ years**.

### 3 · CATMINT — "More Flowers the Less You Fuss"

[LOOP — callback later at #3 in act — light tease only]

[VISUAL: catmint spilling over path edge, bees on lavender-blue spikes]

Lazy gardener gold: cascading mounds, deer-resistant, drought-tolerant once established. Cut back one-third after the first bloom flush — one trim — and it reblooms harder. Zones **3–9**. **$6–10** → **10–15 years** of color.

### 4 · LAVENDER — "Kill It With Kindness"

[VISUAL: lavender on dry gravel, patio seating nearby]

The only reliable way to kill lavender? **Overwatering.** Poor soil, full sun, forget it exists. Perfect near patios. Zones **5–9** (Zone 4: grow in a pot, wheel inside for winter). **$8–14** → structure and scent for **10+ years**.

## [CHAPTER 4:30] ACT II — SUN LOVERS THAT FORGIVE YOU
### *(Four plants for hot, dry, "I forgot to water" yards)*

[VISUAL: "ACT II · SUN & FORGIVE"]

### 5 · BLACK-EYED SUSAN — "Cheerful and Self-Seeding"

[VISUAL: golden rudbeckia field, birds on winter seed heads]

Native, self-seeds politely, feeds birds if you skip deadheading. **First year:** don't fertilize for blooms — let roots build. **$5–9**. Zones **3–9**. **10–12 years** minimum.

### 6 · CONEFLOWER (Echinacea) — "The Taproot Trick"

[LOOP — PAYOFF tease from hook — sedum was #1 in sedum act; coneflower gets June frame]

[VISUAL: purple coneflower, finger checking soil depth]

Advertised as drought-tolerant — **true after year one.** New plants need the 2-inch finger test: dry? Deep water. Moist? Wait. That builds the taproot that makes coneflowers bulletproof. Full sun, poor soil OK. Zones **3–9**. **$5–10** → **10–15 years**.

### 7 · YARROW — "Water Less, Not More"

[VISUAL: white yarrow in rocky lean soil]

Opposite of most plants: **overwatering** kills new yarrow. Water every 7–10 days when dry 2 inches down. Lean soil = upright blooms. Zones **3–9**. **$5–9**. **10–15 years**.

### 8 · COREOPSIS (Tickseed) — "Self-Cleaning Sunshine"

[VISUAL: yellow coreopsis border, long-season bloom]

Many modern varieties drop spent flowers themselves — no deadheading. Heat and drought tolerant. Zones **3–9**. **$6–10**. Nonstop yellow from late spring to frost once established.

## [CHAPTER 7:45] ACT III — SHADE WITHOUT THE WORK
### *(Two problem-solvers for dark yards)*

[VISUAL: "ACT III · SHADE FIX"]

### 9 · HOSTA — "The Shade Workhorse"

[VISUAL: blue-green hosta under trees, dramatic foliage close-up]

Shade plants fail in June because **surface soil looks wet while roots are dry**. First summer: check 6 inches down, water deeply when dry. After that? Hands off for **20–30 years**. Zones **3–9**. **$8–15** (common varieties).

### 10 · LIRIOPE (Lilyturf) — "The Ugly Spot Fixer"

[VISUAL: liriope mat under mature tree, purple flower spikes]

Dry, root-choked soil under trees? Liriope forms a weed-blocking mat. Shade or sun. Not flashy — **useful**. Zones **5–10**. **$6–9** per division, spreads into free plants.

Four-season structure. Late winter: cut to 6 inches. Done until next year. Zones **4–9**. **$12–18** → **15+ years** of vertical drama.

### 12 · RUSSIAN SAGE — "Mediterranean Vibes, Midwest Tough"

[VISUAL: silvery-blue spires in wind]

Nursery plants need water twice a week for **6 weeks**, then taper off. Year two: **never irrigate again** or it flops. Full sun, rocky soil. Zones **4–9**. **$8–15**.

### 13 · CREEPING PHLOX — "Spring Carpet, Zero Drama"

[VISUAL: pink phlox cascading over rock wall]

One trim after spring bloom. Holds slopes, crowds weeds. Zones **3–9**. **$8–12**. Maximum visual impact per minute of labor.

### 14 · PEONY — "Plant Once, Bloom for Decades"

The closer. Fall planting. **Pink eyes no more than 2 inches below soil** — too deep = no blooms for years. First June/July after fall plant: weekly deep water. Real show starts **year three**. Documented cases: **50–100+ years** from one root. **$15–35** bare root → generational garden.

## [CHAPTER 11:45] POST-LIST PIVOT — THE JUNE MISTAKE (ONCE, NOT 14 TIMES)

[VISUAL: wilted new perennial vs thriving established neighbor plant split screen]

Fourteen plants. One rule that kills more lazy gardeners than drought, cold, or neglect combined:

**Treating a new perennial like an established one.**

Every plant on this list earns its "low maintenance" reputation **after** one full summer — sometimes two. That first June/July, roots are shallow. They need deep water when dry — not a sprinkle, not "it's drought tolerant so I'll ignore it."

From **year two forward**, you can actually be lazy. That's the deal.

### BONUS: 3 LAZY GARDENER RULES

**RULE 1 — Deep, not often.** Soak to 6–8 inches once a week in year one. Not daily sprinkles.

**RULE 2 — Mulch once in spring.** 2–3 inches of bark or compost. Cuts watering stress and weeds. Lazy multiplier.

**RULE 3 — Never fertilize new plants for extra blooms.** Roots first, flowers second. Year two explodes.
